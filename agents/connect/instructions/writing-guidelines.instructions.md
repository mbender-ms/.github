# Writing Guidelines

Follow these guidelines when writing impact statements and supporting activity descriptions for the Connect document.

---

## Think in "Big Rocks"

Do not list every minor task performed during the period. Instead, identify the **big rocks** — the major deliverables, initiatives, or outcomes that made a meaningful difference to the business.

Group related smaller activities under the big rock they support. Activities are evidence for impact, not standalone items.

## Impact-First Framing

Lead each item with the **impact** (the outcome or result), then follow with the supporting activities that produced it.

- **Good:** "Reduced content build errors by 40% by redesigning the validation pipeline, which unblocked three downstream publishing teams."
- **Avoid:** "Redesigned the validation pipeline. Fixed bugs. Updated documentation."

## Measurable vs. Qualitative Impact

Prefer measurable outcomes when available (metrics, percentages, counts, before/after comparisons).

When a measurable outcome is not available, use qualitative evidence:
- Positive feedback from customers or internal stakeholders.
- Adoption signals (e.g., other teams started using the tool).
- Anecdotal evidence that the activity was worth pursuing.

Even when impact is implied rather than explicitly measured, include the item and frame the value clearly.

## Collaborative Framing

Wherever possible, describe how the work:
- **Built on the accomplishments of others** — leveraged existing tools, frameworks, or prior art from colleagues.
- **Contributed to the success of others** — enabled a teammate, partner team, or customer to achieve their goals.

Impact should not be presented as purely individual. Show how the user's work fits into a larger collaborative effort.

## Stretch Activity Framing

Stretch items (`[STRETCH]`) represent above-and-beyond contributions outside the user's primary role class. Frame them to highlight:
- Why the contribution was outside normal scope.
- The specific value it created beyond the expected baseline.
- How it benefited the team, org, or customers.

## Tone and Style

- Professional but not stiff. Write as a confident peer, not a formal report.
- Concise. Every sentence should earn its place.
- Active voice preferred.
- Avoid jargon unless it is meaningful to the audience (the user's manager and leadership).
