# World-Builder — Hard Sci-Fi Setting Skill

Create scientifically grounded, emotionally resonant settings for space opera romance novels.

## When to Use

- Designing a new story universe from scratch
- Creating specific locations (stations, ships, planets, academies)
- Designing technology systems (FTL, weapons, communication, medicine)
- Building political structures (factions, governments, military hierarchies)
- Creating alien species or post-human variants
- Ensuring scientific consistency across the manuscript

---

## Design Philosophy

### The Maas Principle: Worlds That Feel
The setting should evoke emotion as powerfully as the characters. A space station isn't just a setting — it's oppressive or liberating, a cage or a sanctuary. Every location has an emotional register.

### The Yarros Principle: Worlds That Threaten
The setting is actively dangerous. The academy can kill you. The ship can fail. Space is trying to kill everyone all the time. This constant danger bonds characters and creates romantic pressure.

### The Hard Sci-Fi Principle: Worlds That Work
Technology has rules, costs, and consequences. The reader should feel that this universe could exist. Internal consistency is non-negotiable.

## World Design Framework

### Layer 1: Physics and Technology

Design the technological backbone first — it constrains everything else.

**FTL Travel** (choose or customize):
| System | Mechanism | Cost/Limitation | Romantic Potential |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fold-Space Drive | Compresses space between two points | Massive energy cost; requires rare fuel; ships are vulnerable during fold | Fuel scarcity drives conflict; fold-navigation requires bonded pair (romance!) |
| Slip-Stream Network | Natural or artificial corridors between star systems | Fixed routes; choke points; some routes are unstable | Strategic control of routes = political power; being stranded together off-network |
| Quantum Tunneling | Instantaneous but limited by mass/distance | Only works for small masses; biological transit causes neural trauma | Personal teleportation but ships travel conventionally; trauma bonds |
| Generation Ships | No FTL — relativistic travel | Time dilation; crews age differently than people at destination | Lovers separated by time; arriving to find decades passed |

**Communication**:
- Light-speed delay is real and matters narratively
- Quantum-entangled comm (ansible) — instantaneous but expensive, limited bandwidth
- FTL courier ships — information travels at plot speed
- Light-delay creates isolation, misunderstanding, and dramatic irony

**Weapons and Defense**:
- Kinetic weapons (railguns, mass drivers) — simple, devastating, Newtonian
- Directed energy (lasers, particle beams) — light-speed accuracy, power-hungry
- Missile/torpedo systems — smart, versatile, counterable
- Shields — energy-intensive, ablative, or frequency-tuned
- Point defense — automated last-resort systems
- **Rule**: Every weapon has a counter. Every defense has a weakness.

**Medicine and Biology**:
- Nanomed swarms for field treatment
- Regeneration tanks for major trauma (hours to days, not instant)
- Genetic augmentation (legal or black-market)
- Psionic abilities (if present) — biological, not magical. Neural architecture differences.
- **Rule**: Healing takes time. Scars persist. The body keeps score.

### Layer 2: Political Geography

**Faction Design Template**:
```markdown
### [Faction Name]

**Territory**: [Star systems, stations, regions controlled]
**Government**: [How are they governed? Military junta, democracy, oligarchy, AI-managed]
**Military**: [Fleet composition, doctrine, special forces]
**Culture**: [Values, aesthetics, social norms, gender dynamics]
**Economy**: [What do they produce, trade, need?]
**Relationship to protagonist**: [Ally, enemy, complicated]
**Internal conflict**: [Every faction has fractures — what are theirs?]
**Romantic relevance**: [How does this faction create romantic pressure or opportunity?]
```

**Political Structures That Create Romance Pressure**:
- Arranged political marriages / command pairings
- Forbidden cross-faction relationships
- Military fraternization rules
- Caste or augmentation hierarchies that separate lovers
- Duty-vs-desire conflicts driven by faction loyalty

### Layer 3: Locations

Every major location needs both a physical and emotional identity.

**Location Design Template**:
```markdown
### [Location Name]

**Type**: [Station / Ship / Planet / Moon / Habitat / Academy]
**Physical**: [Size, layout, environment, distinctive features]
**Sensory palette**: [What does it look, sound, smell, feel like?]
**Emotional register**: [What does it feel like to be here? Claustrophobic? Free? Dangerous? Sacred?]
**Inhabitants**: [Who lives/works here? Demographics, culture]
**Danger level**: [What can kill you here?]
**Key scenes**: [What kinds of scenes happen best in this location?]
**Romantic potential**: [Private alcoves? Dangerous perimeter? Forced-proximity quarters? Zero-g observation decks with starfield views?]
```

**Location Types That Drive Romance**:

| Location | Why It Works |
|---|---|
| **Fleet Academy** | Forced proximity, shared danger, training bonds, competitive tension, barracks intimacy |
| **Warship bridge** | Power dynamics, professional proximity, life-or-death trust, command bond |
| **Space station underbelly** | Secrets, hidden meetings, danger, the thrill of forbidden spaces |
| **Observation deck** | Isolation, beauty, vulnerability — the stars as romantic backdrop |
| **Med bay** | One character injured, the other hovering. Vulnerability stripped bare. |
| **Zero-g environment** | Bodies floating, disoriented, catching each other — physical intimacy disguised as necessity |
| **Alien world surface** | Wonder, danger, discovery — sharing something no one else has seen |
| **Escape pod / damaged shuttle** | Ultimate forced proximity. Limited air. Nothing to do but talk (or not talk). |

### Layer 4: Social Systems

Design the social fabric characters navigate:

- **Military rank structure**: Creates power dynamics for romance
- **Augmentation hierarchy**: Who's enhanced, who's baseline, who's prejudiced
- **Bond systems**: Neural links, command pairs, psionic bonds — the sci-fi "mating bond"
- **Education/training pipeline**: The academy-to-fleet path
- **Economic class**: Station-born vs. planet-born, core vs. rim
- **Cultural attitudes toward romance**: Public or private? Encouraged or discouraged?

## Scientific Consistency Checklist

When designing or writing, verify:

- [ ] **Orbital mechanics**: Ships don't hover. Orbits are specific. Delta-v matters.
- [ ] **Thermal management**: Ships generate heat. Waste heat must be radiated. Stealth in space is hard.
- [ ] **Acceleration effects**: Humans feel g-forces. Sustained thrust at 1g = comfortable. 3g = brutal. 10g = fatal without support.
- [ ] **Vacuum exposure**: Unconsciousness in ~15 seconds. Death in ~90 seconds. No explosive decompression.
- [ ] **Communication delay**: Light takes 8 minutes from Sun to Earth. Even "nearby" stars are light-years apart.
- [ ] **Radiation**: Space is radioactive. Ships need shielding. Solar events are dangerous.
- [ ] **Resource scarcity**: Air, water, food, fuel — all finite on a ship. Resupply matters.
- [ ] **Sound in space**: There isn't any. Inside ships, yes. Outside, silence.

**Soft exceptions allowed**: If you establish a rule early (e.g., "the neural link creates an empathic sense between bonded pairs"), you can use it consistently. But acknowledge that it's at the edge of known science.

## Anti-Patterns

- ❌ Magical technology with no rules or costs
- ❌ Info-dump worldbuilding chapters — weave it through character experience
- ❌ Earth-with-spaceships — the setting should feel genuinely alien and vast
- ❌ Technology that removes all danger — the world must threaten the characters
- ❌ Ignoring the body in space — zero-g, radiation, acceleration are constant companions
- ❌ Monoculture planets/factions — every group has diversity, dissent, and internal politics
