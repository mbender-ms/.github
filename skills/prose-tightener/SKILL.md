# Prose-Tightener — Line Editing Skill

Turn good prose into publication-ready prose. Cut what doesn't serve. Strengthen what remains.

## When to Use

- A chapter is drafted and needs line-level tightening
- Pacing is uneven — scenes drag or rush
- Dialogue sounds samey between characters
- Prose feels flabby — too many words for too little impact
- Chapter openings or closings need more hook
- Preparing a manuscript for final submission

---

## The Tightening Hierarchy

Edit in this order. Fixing a higher-level problem often eliminates lower-level ones automatically.

### Level 1: Scene-Level Cuts (Biggest Impact)
Before tightening sentences, ask whether entire scenes or passages earn their place.

**Cut candidates:**
- Scenes that only do one thing (only advance plot, only develop character — good scenes do 2-3)
- "Getting ready" scenes — characters traveling, preparing, waiting without tension or development
- Repeated information — the reader already knows this; the character already processed this
- Scenes where the character reaches a conclusion the reader reached chapters ago
- Flashbacks that tell rather than deepen

**Rule of thumb**: If you can summarize a scene in one sentence and nothing is lost, the scene should probably be one sentence (a transitional line) or cut entirely.

### Level 2: Paragraph-Level Tightening
Each paragraph should have one clear purpose.

**Tighten by:**
- Merging paragraphs that make the same point
- Cutting the "warm-up" sentence at the start of paragraphs — often the real start is the second sentence
- Removing paragraphs that repeat what dialogue already conveyed
- Cutting descriptive paragraphs that pause the action without adding atmosphere or character

### Level 3: Sentence-Level Tightening
Where line editing lives.

**The 30% Rule**: Most first-draft sentences can lose 30% of their words and become stronger.

**Before:**
> I could feel the cold air of the station's recycling system as it blew across my face and down the corridor, making me shiver involuntarily as I walked toward the briefing room.

**After:**
> Station air bit my face — recycled, metallic, cold enough to raise goosebumps. I pulled my jacket tighter and kept walking.

**What changed:**
- Removed filter word ("I could feel")
- Made the air an active subject
- Added specific sensory detail (metallic)
- Replaced told reaction ("making me shiver involuntarily") with shown reaction (pulling jacket)
- Split into two sentences for rhythm variety

### Level 4: Word-Level Precision
Individual word choices that sharpen or soften impact.

## Line Editing Rules

### Rule 1: Kill Filter Words
Filter words place a barrier between the reader and the experience.

| ❌ Filter | ✅ Direct |
|-----------|----------|
| I felt the ship shudder | The ship shuddered beneath my feet |
| I could see the fleet approaching | The fleet resolved on the display — twelve signatures, closing fast |
| I noticed he was tense | His jaw was wired shut, hands white-knuckled on the armrests |
| I heard the alarm | The alarm split the silence — three sharp bursts |
| I thought about what she said | Her words circled back, relentless |
| I realized the truth | The truth landed like a gut punch |

**Common filters to search and destroy**: felt, noticed, heard, saw, watched, realized, thought, wondered, knew, seemed, appeared, decided, began to, started to, tried to.

### Rule 2: Verb Strength
Strong, specific verbs carry more weight than weak verbs + adverbs.

| ❌ Weak | ✅ Strong |
|---------|----------|
| walked quickly | strode / rushed / charged |
| said quietly | murmured / whispered / breathed |
| looked at angrily | glared / pinned with a look |
| moved carefully | crept / edged / picked her way |
| hit hard | slammed / cracked / drove into |

**Rule**: If you need an adverb, the verb is probably wrong.

### Rule 3: Eliminate Weasel Words
Words that add length without meaning:

- **just**: "I just wanted to say" → "I wanted to say" (or cut the whole phrase)
- **really, very, quite, rather, somewhat**: Almost always cuttable
- **that**: Often unnecessary. "She knew that he was lying" → "She knew he was lying"
- **began to, started to**: "She began to run" → "She ran" (unless the interruption is the point)
- **there was/were**: "There were three ships on the display" → "Three ships glowed on the display"
- **it was**: "It was dark in the corridor" → "The corridor swallowed the light"

### Rule 4: Vary Sentence Structure
Monotonous rhythm kills pacing.

**Bad — Same structure repeated:**
> I walked to the bridge. I checked the display. I saw the fleet. I gripped the console. I turned to Kael.

**Good — Varied structure:**
> The bridge was three decks up and a lifetime away. I checked the display — twelve signatures, holding formation. The fleet. My grip on the console turned my knuckles white, and when I turned, Kael was already watching me.

**Rhythm tools:**
- Fragment for punch: "Gone. All of it."
- Long sentence for tension: Pile clause on clause to mirror building dread
- Short after long for impact: Follow a complex sentence with a gut-punch simple one
- Vary paragraph length: One-sentence paragraphs for emphasis. Longer paragraphs for immersion.

### Rule 5: Dialogue Sharpening

**Cut:**
- Greetings and small talk (unless they reveal character or tension)
- Characters saying each other's names mid-conversation (people rarely do this)
- Complete sentences where fragments feel natural: "You coming?" not "Are you coming with us?"
- Dialogue that restates what the reader already knows

**Sharpen:**
- Each character's speech should be identifiable without tags
- Subtext: What they mean vs. what they say should diverge
- Interruptions, trailing off, topic changes — real speech patterns
- The best dialogue lines can stand alone as memorable quotes

### Rule 6: Show-Don't-Tell Enforcement

| ❌ Telling | ✅ Showing |
|-----------|----------|
| She was nervous | Her fingers drummed the console. Stopped. Started again. |
| He was attracted to her | His gaze snagged on her mouth. He looked away too fast. |
| They were exhausted | Kael's head dropped against the bulkhead. Neither of them spoke for a long time. |
| The station was enormous | The viewport filled with it — an arc of steel and light that swallowed the stars. |
| She was angry | "Don't." One word. A blade. |

### Rule 7: Chapter Hooks

**Opening hooks** — The first line of every chapter must compel the reader forward:
- Start with action, sensation, or charged dialogue — never setting description or backstory
- Orient quickly but don't over-explain — trust the reader
- If continuing a cliffhanger, deliver on the tension immediately (don't deflect with a POV switch to a peaceful scene)

**Closing hooks** — The last line of every chapter must make putting the book down painful:
- End on a revelation, reversal, question, threat, or emotional gut-punch
- The best last lines are short
- Never end on a character going to sleep (unless something wakes them)
- Never end on resolution — always leave tension unspent

## Pacing Fixes

### Scene Drags
**Symptoms**: Reader wants to skim. Nothing feels urgent.
**Causes**: Too much description, internal monologue that circles, action without stakes
**Fixes**:
- Cut the first 20% of the scene — it's usually warm-up
- Add a ticking clock (literal or metaphorical)
- Give the POV character an active goal in the scene
- Cut transitions between sub-scenes — jump cut to the interesting part

### Scene Rushes
**Symptoms**: Big moments feel weightless. Emotional beats don't land.
**Causes**: Not enough space for the reader to feel, plot moving too fast
**Fixes**:
- Slow down by adding sensory detail and internal reaction
- Give emotional moments their own paragraph (or their own beat)
- Use white space (paragraph breaks) to create pause
- Extend dialogue in charged moments — let characters struggle to say hard things

```markdown
| Location | Current | Issue | Recommended |
|----------|---------|-------|-------------|

### Pacing Notes
- [Section]: [Drags/rushes — specific fix]

### Dialogue Notes
- [Character]: [Voice issue or sharpening opportunity]

### Strong Lines (Keep)
- "[quoted text]" — [Why it works]

### Word Count
- Current: [X]
- After recommended cuts: [~Y]
- Reduction: [~Z%]
```

## Anti-Patterns

- ❌ Over-tightening emotional moments — sometimes lingering IS the point. Don't cut the breath.
- ❌ Making every sentence short and punchy — varied rhythm is the goal, not uniform brevity
- ❌ Removing voice for efficiency — the narrator's personality must survive editing
- ❌ Cutting all adverbs dogmatically — "she said softly" is fine if the softness matters and no action beat works better
- ❌ Tightening prose before fixing structural issues — don't polish sentences in a scene that might get cut
- ❌ Applying newspaper prose standards to fiction — fiction needs beauty and rhythm alongside efficiency
