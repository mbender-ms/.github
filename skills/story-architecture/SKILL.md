# Story-Architecture — Plot Structure Skill

Design narrative architecture for romantic hard sci-fi novels — from series-level arcs down to individual chapter beats.

## When to Use

- Planning a new novel or series from concept to chapter outline
- Creating a beat sheet from a rough premise
- Outlining individual chapters with scene goals
- Designing series-level arcs across multiple books
- Mapping romantic progression alongside external plot

---

## The Dual-Engine Story Model

Every book runs on two engines simultaneously:

### Engine 1: The External Plot (Space Opera)
The large-scale conflict that drives action, stakes, and worldbuilding:
- Fleet warfare, interstellar politics, alien contact, precursor mysteries
- Escalates across the series — each book raises the stakes
- Provides the *crucible* that forces characters together and tests their bond

### Engine 2: The Internal Plot (Romance)
The emotional/romantic arc that drives character growth and reader investment:
- Follows established romance beats (see Romance Beat Sheet below)
- Escalates from tension → trust → intimacy → crisis → resolution
- Provides the *reason to care* about the external conflict

**The Rule**: Neither engine runs without the other. The romance creates emotional stakes for the action. The action creates pressure that accelerates the romance. They are symbiotic.

## Three-Act Structure (Single Book)

### Act I — The Crucible (0–25%)

| Beat | Description | Romance Layer |
|---|---|---|
| **Opening Image** | The protagonist's world before everything changes. Establish normal — then break it. | Show what's missing emotionally. Loneliness, duty without joy, survival without living. |
| **Inciting Incident** (5-10%) | The event that forces the protagonist into the story. Orders, discovery, attack, selection. | The love interest enters the story — or becomes unavoidable. First impression is charged (antagonism, attraction, or both). |
| **Refusal / Resistance** | The protagonist resists the new situation. They have reasons to stay in their lane. | Active resistance to the attraction. Denial. Rationalization. "I don't have time for this." |
| **Crossing the Threshold** (20-25%) | The protagonist commits — or is forced — into the new world. No going back. | Forced proximity. They're stuck together: co-pilots, same squad, shared mission, command pair. |

### Act II-A — Rising Pressure (25–50%)

| Beat | Description | Romance Layer |
|---|---|---|
| **New World / Training** | The protagonist learns the rules of their new reality. Allies, enemies, systems. | Grudging respect develops. The love interest proves competent, brave, or unexpectedly kind. |
| **Fun and Games** | The "promise of the premise." Space battles, training montages, exploration, heists. | The banter phase. Verbal sparring. Accidental touches. Shared glances that last too long. The slow burn ignites. |
| **B-Story Deepening** | Secondary characters and subplots develop. Found family forms. | Jealousy, rivals, past relationships surface. The love interest's walls show cracks. |
| **Midpoint Shift** (50%) | A major revelation or victory that changes everything. False victory or false defeat. | The First Real Moment — a kiss, a confession, a bond activation, a moment of unguarded vulnerability that can't be taken back. |

### Act II-B — The Descent (50–75%)

| Beat | Description | Romance Layer |
|---|---|---|
| **Consequences** | The midpoint shift creates fallout. Enemies respond. Allies fracture. | The relationship deepens but complications multiply. External forces conspire to separate them. |
| **Enemies Close In** | The external threat escalates. Betrayals, losses, impossible choices. | The love interest's secret or past is revealed. Trust is tested. |
| **Dark Night of the Soul** (65-75%) | All is lost. The protagonist has lost — or believes they've lost — everything that matters. | The Dark Moment: separation, betrayal, sacrifice, or apparent death. The relationship shatters (or seems to). |

### Act III — The Reckoning (75–100%)

| Beat | Description | Romance Layer |
|---|---|---|
| **Break into Three** | The protagonist finds new resolve. A key insight, alliance, or ability unlocks the path forward. | The protagonist realizes what (who) truly matters. Chooses love and fight, not love or fight. |
| **Climax** (85-95%) | The final battle, confrontation, or crisis. Maximum stakes. | The romantic reunion — fighting together, choosing each other publicly, the bond fully activated. Love is the force multiplier. |
| **Resolution** (95-100%) | New equilibrium. The world has changed. The protagonist has changed. | For standalones: HEA or HFN. For series: resolution of THIS book's romantic crisis, but seeding the next book's tension. |
| **Final Image** | Mirror of the opening — showing how far the protagonist has come. | Emotional bookend. The loneliness from page one is answered. |

## Romance Beat Sheet

These are the core romantic beats that must appear in every book. Timing is flexible, but sequence matters.

| # | Beat | Timing | Description |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **First Encounter** | Act I | Charged first meeting. Antagonism, instant attraction, or both. First impressions are wrong (or devastatingly right). |
| 2 | **Forced Proximity** | Act I / Act II-A | Circumstances force them together. Same ship, same squad, paired by command. Can't escape each other. |
| 3 | **The Spark** | Act II-A | First moment of unexpected connection. A laugh. A saved life. A vulnerability revealed. The walls flicker. |
| 4 | **Banter Phase** | Act II-A | Verbal sparring becomes the language of attraction. Each exchange reveals more than intended. |
| 5 | **The Almost** | Act II-A | A near-kiss, near-confession, near-surrender — interrupted. Tension ratchets higher. |
| 6 | **The Surrender** | Midpoint | First kiss, first intimate moment, bond activation. Something that can't be undone. |
| 7 | **Deepening** | Act II-B | Emotional and/or physical intimacy deepens. Secrets shared. Vulnerabilities exposed. |
| 8 | **The Reveal** | Act II-B | A secret, past, or loyalty conflict surfaces. Trust fractures. |
| 9 | **Dark Moment** | Act II-B | Separation — physical, emotional, or both. The relationship appears doomed. |
| 10 | **Grand Gesture / Reunion** | Act III | One or both characters choose the other decisively. Public, costly, irreversible. |
| 11 | **Resolution** | Act III | Relationship stabilizes at a new level. HEA, HFN, or devastating cliffhanger (series). |

## Series Arc Design (Trilogy / Multi-Book)

### Book 1: Discovery
- **External**: Introduce the world, the threat, the stakes. The protagonist is thrust into a new reality.
- **Romance**: Enemies-to-allies-to-first-kiss. The bond sparks but isn't resolved.
- **Ends with**: A victory that reveals a larger threat. Romantic promise, not resolution.

### Book 2: Escalation
- **External**: The scope expands. New factions, deeper lore, higher stakes. The threat is worse than anyone knew.
- **Romance**: Full relationship exploration. Deepest intimacy AND deepest crisis. The Dark Moment of the series.
- **Ends with**: A devastating loss, separation, or betrayal. The reader should be wrecked.

### Book 3: Reckoning
- **External**: All-out war, final confrontation, universe-level stakes. Everything built across the series pays off.
- **Romance**: Reunion, reconciliation, fighting as a bonded pair. The relationship is the secret weapon.
- **Ends with**: Resolution of all threads. HEA or bittersweet victory. The emotional payoff of three books.

## Chapter Outline Format

When creating chapter outlines, use this format:

```markdown
## Chapter [N] — [Title]

**POV**: [Character name]
**Location**: [Setting]
**Timeline**: [When this happens relative to previous chapter]

### Scene Goals
- **Plot**: [What happens — external action]
- **Romance**: [Relationship beat — where are they on the romance arc?]
- **Character**: [Internal growth — what does the POV character learn or confront?]

### Key Beats
1. [Opening hook]
2. [Rising action / escalation]
3. [Turn / pivot moment]
4. [Emotional landing]
5. [Chapter-end hook]

### Notes
- [Worldbuilding details to weave in]
- [Callbacks to earlier chapters]
- [Foreshadowing to plant]
```

## Cliffhanger Engineering

Every chapter ending falls into one of these categories:

| Type | Example | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **The Revelation** | "The signal wasn't a distress call. It was a countdown." | Plot-heavy chapters |
| **The Arrival** | A new character, ship, or threat appears in the final line | Mid-act transitions |
| **The Decision** | The protagonist commits to a course that can't be reversed | Character-turning chapters |
| **The Emotional Bomb** | A confession, betrayal, or loss delivered in the final beat | Romance-heavy chapters |
| **The Physical Threat** | An explosion, attack, or disaster in progress — scene cuts mid-action | Action chapters |
| **The Question** | An unanswered question that the reader must turn the page to resolve | Mystery/intrigue chapters |

---

## Anti-Patterns

- ❌ Romance that pauses during action arcs — both engines always run
- ❌ External plot that resolves without the relationship mattering to the outcome
- ❌ Dark Moments that feel manufactured — they must grow organically from character flaws and world pressures
- ❌ Chapters that exist only for worldbuilding or only for romance — every chapter serves both
- ❌ Series books that don't work as standalone emotional arcs — each book needs its own satisfying shape
