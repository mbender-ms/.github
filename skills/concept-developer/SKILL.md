# Concept Developer — Premise Expansion Skill

Take a chosen story concept and build it into a complete premise document. This is the bridge between "I want this" and "I'm ready to outline."

---

## When to Use

- The user has chosen a concept from brainstorming and wants it fully developed
- A partial premise needs to be stress-tested and expanded before outlining
- An existing story idea needs to be formalized into a structured document
- The brainstorm agent invokes this after concept selection

---

## Output: The Premise Document

The premise document covers eight sections. Complete all eight before handing off to any downstream agent.

---

### Section 1: The Logline

One sentence. No more. Format:

*[PROTAGONIST] must [EXTERNAL GOAL] before [STAKES/DEADLINE], but [INTERNAL OBSTACLE/COMPLICATION].*

The logline is the truth of the book. If it takes two sentences, the concept has two centers and needs to choose one.

---

### Section 2: Protagonist Profile

**Name**: Working name (can be changed). Avoid placeholder names — commit to something, it helps with thinking.

**Age & Background**: Where did she come from? What shaped her before page one?

**External Goal**: What does she want in the story? This is what she'd say if you asked her.

**Internal Need**: What does she actually need? This is what the story gives her whether she wants it or not.

**The Wound**: The specific past event or condition that created her internal obstacle. Specific beats general — "her mother abandoned her" is weaker than "she was the one who found her mother's note, and she was nine, and she burned it before her brother could read it."

**Voice Register**: How does she think? What's her internal language? (analytical, sensory, sarcastic, precise, emotional, mythological, etc.)

**The Ability/Power/Skill**: If she has a supernatural ability, power system, or extraordinary competence — define it precisely. What can she do, what can't she do, what does it cost?

---

### Section 3: World Foundation

**Setting**: Where and when. City, region, era, social context. Be specific — "Seattle, present day, in the shadow of the tech boom, where indigenous place-names mark a landscape the newcomers don't understand" is better than "contemporary Seattle."

**The Supernatural/SFF Layer**: What exists in this world that doesn't exist in ours? How is it structured? Who knows about it? How long has it been there?

**The Rules**: Three to five non-negotiable rules of this world. These create the constraints that make plot possible.

**The Texture**: What does this world smell like, sound like, feel like? Two to three sensory details that ground it. These become the prose's recurring imagery.

**What's Hidden**: Every good world has a layer the protagonist doesn't know yet. What does the reader eventually learn that reframes everything?

---

### Section 4: Central Conflict Architecture

**The External Conflict**: What is the plot? What is threatening, broken, or in motion that requires the protagonist to act?

**The Internal Conflict**: What is the protagonist's arc? What does she have to become or accept or release to survive the story?

**The Link**: How are the external and internal conflicts connected? The best stories make these the same problem in two registers — solving one requires solving the other.

**The Antagonist**: Who or what opposes the protagonist? Antagonists work best when they represent what the protagonist could become if she chose wrong, or what she fears she already is.

**The Ticking Clock**: What creates urgency? Why can't the protagonist wait?

---

### Section 5: Romance Architecture

**The Love Interest**: Who are they? What makes them specifically right and specifically wrong for the protagonist at this moment in her life?

**The Shape**: What romance trope architecture is this? (enemies-to-lovers, forbidden, second chance, forced proximity, rivals, protector/protected, etc.) Name it — this is not a constraint but a contract with the reader.

**The Obstacle**: What keeps them apart? Must be character-driven, not circumstantial. Circumstances change; character resists.

**The Progression**: Three beats — (1) when the reader first suspects they're going to fall, (2) the moment it becomes undeniable, (3) what has to break open for them to choose each other.

**The Payoff**: What does the relationship give the protagonist that the plot can't? What does she become because of him that she couldn't become alone?

---

### Section 6: Thematic Core

**The Question**: Every story asks a question it doesn't answer directly. What is this story asking? ("What do we owe the dead?" / "Can you be whole if you were built in pieces?" / "Is protection the same as love?")

**The Argument**: What position does the story ultimately take? (The answer the protagonist's arc embodies at the end.)

**The Image**: One recurring image or motif that carries the theme — something physical and specific that appears at the beginning, middle, and end in evolving form.

---

### Section 7: Series Potential

**Standalone vs. Series**: Does this concept have series legs? What expands across books?

**Book 1 Scope**: What question does Book 1 answer? What does it leave open?

**The Arc**: If series, sketch the shape — what does the protagonist's journey look like across 3–4 books? Where does she end?

**The World's Expansion**: What layer of the world does each book peel back?

---

### Section 8: Comp Titles & Market Position

**Primary Comps**: Two to three published books this most resembles. Be honest and specific.

**The Differentiator**: One sentence on what this book does that the comps don't. This is the pitch.

**Reader Promise**: What does a reader who picks this up expect to feel by the last page? What experience are you promising them?

---

## Quality Checks

Before delivering the premise document, verify:

- [ ] The logline is one sentence and names the real story
- [ ] The protagonist's wound is specific, not generic
- [ ] The external and internal conflicts are linked, not parallel
- [ ] The romance obstacle is character-driven
- [ ] The world has at least one hidden layer
- [ ] The thematic question is a real question, not a thesis statement
- [ ] The comp titles are honest

---

## Handoff Options

After delivering the premise document:

**→ story-architecture**: If the user is ready to build a beat sheet and chapter outline
**→ world-builder**: If the world needs significant development before outlining can begin
**→ character-forge**: If the protagonist or love interest needs deeper development before the story can be structured
**→ back to brainstorm agent**: If developing the concept revealed it has problems that need a new concept or significant pivot
