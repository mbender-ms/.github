# Scifi-World-Review — Worldbuilding Evaluation Skill

Evaluate the story's sci-fi worldbuilding with the critical eye of someone who's read the best the genre offers.

## When to Use

- After a full read-through to do a deep worldbuilding assessment
- Evaluating specific world systems (FTL, factions, technology) for depth and plausibility
- Comparing the world against genre comp titles
- Testing whether the worldbuilding would satisfy a dedicated sci-fi audience
- Identifying worldbuilding gaps before they become reader complaints

---

## Evaluation Framework

### Dimension 1: Plausibility

Does the science hold up? Does the technology feel real?

| Rating | Label | Description |
|--------|-------|-------------|
| 5 | **Hard as diamond** | Real physics respected everywhere. Extrapolated tech is grounded and consistent. Could publish in Analog. |
| 4 | **Solid** | Mostly rigorous. A few soft spots but nothing that breaks trust. Readers nod along. |
| 3 | **Acceptable** | Handwaves some physics but has internal rules. Space opera standard — not hard, but not insulting. |
| 2 | **Soft** | Technology operates on plot convenience. Rules exist but bend frequently. |
| 1 | **Fantasy in a spacesuit** | Science is ignored. Tech is magic. Sci-fi readers will bounce. |

**What to examine:**
- FTL system: rules, costs, limitations, consistency
- Communication: does light-delay exist? If ansible, what are limits?
- Weapons and combat: do they follow any physics?
- Biology: do characters' bodies respond realistically to space environments?
- Energy: where does power come from? Is it finite?
- Materials: are ship/station sizes plausible given materials science?

### Dimension 2: Originality

Has the reader seen this before? What's genuinely new?

| Rating | Label | Description |
|--------|-------|-------------|
| 5 | **Paradigm-shifting** | A world concept I've never encountered. Changes how I think about the genre. |
| 4 | **Fresh** | Familiar elements combined in new ways. Several genuinely original ideas. |
| 3 | **Competent** | Solid execution of established tropes. Nothing new but nothing embarrassing. |
| 2 | **Derivative** | Recognizable borrowing from specific properties. "This is just X with Y." |
| 1 | **Generic** | Could be any space opera. No distinctive identity. |

**What to examine:**
- What's the world's "hook" — the one thing that makes it different from every other space opera?
- Which elements feel genuinely fresh vs. standard-issue?
- Are familiar tropes (chosen one, lost colony, AI rebellion, etc.) executed with a new angle?
- Does the world have an aesthetic identity? Could you recognize it from a description?

### Dimension 3: Depth

Is this a world or a backdrop?

| Rating | Label | Description |
|--------|-------|-------------|
| 5 | **Iceberg** | The story shows 10%, implies 90%. The world extends beyond the page in every direction. |
| 4 | **Layered** | Multiple systems interacting. History, culture, economics feel real. Unexplored areas beckon. |
| 3 | **Adequate** | Enough world for the story. Some depth but edges are visible. |
| 2 | **Thin** | World exists only where the characters walk. Step off the path and there's nothing. |
| 1 | **Wallpaper** | Setting is decoration. Could swap it for a different genre and the story wouldn't change. |

**What to examine:**
- Do factions have history, culture, internal politics?
- Does the economy make sense? Who produces what? Who trades with whom?
- Are there references to things beyond the immediate story? Other cultures, distant events, unexplored regions?
- Does the world feel like it existed before page one and will continue after the last page?

### Dimension 4: Integration

Does the world shape the story, or is it just scenery?

| Rating | Label | Description |
|--------|-------|-------------|
| 5 | **Inseparable** | This story could ONLY happen in this world. Remove the setting and the story collapses. |
| 4 | **Integral** | The world drives major plot points and shapes character decisions. |
| 3 | **Connected** | Some plot elements depend on the world. Others could happen anywhere. |
| 2 | **Loose** | The world is mostly atmosphere. Plot could be transplanted to a different setting. |
| 1 | **Disconnected** | The story ignores its own world. Characters act like they're not in space. |

**What to examine:**
- Do the constraints of the world (FTL limits, communication delay, resource scarcity) actually affect the plot?
- Do characters make decisions that only make sense in this universe?
- Would the romance arc work the same way in a contemporary setting? (If yes, the world isn't integrated enough.)
- Does the climax depend on established world rules?

### Dimension 5: Internal Consistency

Do the rules stay the same throughout?

| Rating | Label | Description |
|--------|-------|-------------|
| 5 | **Airtight** | Every rule established is followed. Exceptions are earned and explained. |
| 4 | **Solid** | Consistent with minor wobbles that don't affect enjoyment. |
| 3 | **Mostly consistent** | A few noticeable contradictions but nothing story-breaking. |
| 2 | **Loose** | Rules bend for plot convenience. Trust is eroding. |
| 1 | **Contradictory** | Rules change between chapters. The world can't be trusted. |

```markdown

**Originality**: [1-5 rating + what's fresh vs. familiar]
```

## Anti-Patterns

- ❌ Demanding Asimov-level rigor from a romance-forward book — calibrate expectations to the genre blend
- ❌ Ignoring emotional worldbuilding — how a world FEELS matters as much as how it WORKS
- ❌ Being pedantic about soft science in a book that never claimed to be hard — check what the book promises
- ❌ Comparing unfavorably to published series without acknowledging those series had multiple books to develop their worlds
- ❌ Forgetting that worldbuilding serves story — a technically perfect world that's boring to read about has failed
